# Task 06 – Energy Dissipation in Repeated Bounces

## Problem Statement

A tennis ball is dropped from height

$$
2.0 \text{ m}
$$

After each bounce, it loses $30\%$ of its mechanical energy.

Determine the height reached after the second bounce.

## Theory

At the highest point after a bounce, the ball's mechanical energy is entirely gravitational potential energy:

$$
E = mgh
$$

If the ball retains $70\%$ of its energy after each bounce, then the maximum height after each bounce is also multiplied by $0.70$.

## Step-by-Step Solution

Initial height:

$$
h_0 = 2.0 \text{ m}
$$

After the first bounce, the ball retains $70\%$ of its energy, so

$$
h_1 = 0.70 h_0
$$

$$
h_1 = 0.70 \cdot 2.0 = 1.4 \text{ m}
$$

After the second bounce, the same factor applies again:

$$
h_2 = 0.70 h_1
$$

$$
h_2 = 0.70 \cdot 1.4
$$

$$
h_2 = 0.98 \text{ m}
$$

Equivalently,

$$
h_2 = 2.0 \cdot (0.70)^2 = 0.98 \text{ m}
$$

## Final Result

The ball rises to

$$
0.98 \text{ m}
$$

after the second bounce.

## Interpretation

Because potential energy at the top is proportional to height, the same energy-retention factor applies directly to the rebound height. Repeated bounces therefore form a geometric sequence of decreasing heights.
